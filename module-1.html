Module 01

The fuel value chain & where a distributor sits

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Trace a gallon of diesel from crude oil all the way to the customer's tank.
  • Name every player in the chain and say what they actually do — and charge for.
  • Pin down precisely what a distributor is and isn't, because that placement defines every constraint on the business.

The chain, concretely

Follow one gallon of ultra-low-sulfur diesel (ULSD) reaching a waste-hauler's truck yard in northern New Jersey.

1 · Crude → Refiner

A refiner — think Phillips 66 Bayway in Linden, NJ — turns crude oil into finished ULSD. Crude is the single biggest cost in the gallon; it regularly accounts for around half of the end price of diesel. Refiners sell to large buyers under term contracts and make up any shortfall by buying on the spot market.

2 · Refiner → Terminal / Rack

Finished fuel moves by pipeline or barge into terminals — bulk storage sites, usually on a pipeline. The loading point where trucks fill up is the rack. The U.S. has roughly 400 racks across more than 1,100 terminals. Northern NJ — the Linden / Bayonne / Newark complex — is one of the densest terminal markets in the country, which is a structural advantage for any distributor based nearby.

3 · Rack → Distributor

This is where the distributor enters. Its trucks (owned or contracted) load at the rack in transport quantity — about 8,000 gallons a load. A distributor is a jobber: a company that resells fuel, for whom fuel is a revenue center rather than an input.

4 · Distributor → End user

The distributor delivers to utilities, construction sites, trucking fleets, and more — either into the customer's bulk storage tank, or directly into equipment (mobile / on-site fueling).

Terminal / Rackstores it, posts a daily price
Distributor (you)buys ~8,000 gal, resells + delivers

The mental model to keep

A distributor buys wholesale at a benchmark-linked price and resells with a thin per-gallon markup plus service value — delivery, reliability, emergency response, tank assets. It owns almost none of the price of the gallon. It owns the movement of it and the customer.

Put differently: a distributor is a margin-thin, logistics-heavy, data-rich intermediary. It can't control crude or rack prices — those are set upstream and are volatile. Its controllable levers are only four: which rack it buys from, how it routes its trucks, how it prices its bids, and how fast it collects its cash. Almost every way to make the business better lives in one of those four buckets — hold onto that, because it recurs in every later module.

Key terms
Downstream
The consumer-facing end of oil: refining, distribution, marketing, retail.
Refiner
Converts crude into finished products. Owns the largest share of the gallon's cost.
Terminal
Bulk storage, usually on a pipeline or waterway, where fuel waits before truck loadout.
Rack
The physical truck-loading point at a terminal — and the name for the wholesale price posted there.
Jobber / distributor / marketer
A company that buys at the rack and resells/delivers to end users. This is the business you work for.
Transport vs. bobtail
A transport is a ~8,000-gallon semi-tanker; a bobtail is a small (~2,000–4,000 gal) truck for last-mile / on-site fills.
Check your understanding
Q1If crude prices spike, who in the chain has the least ability to mark it up — and what happens to a distributor caught mid-stream?show answer
Nobody in the middle truly absorbs a spike — it gets passed through to the end user fast (rack resets that evening). The real exposure is the lag: a distributor delivering on a fixed quote or contract while paying today's higher cost gets whipsawed. So the middle of the chain doesn't avoid price moves — it gets caught by the timing of them. That's exactly why floating (rack-plus) contracts and hedging exist.
Q2Name two distinct ways a northern-NJ location near the terminals is a competitive moat.show answer
(1) Lower haul-to-rack cost — fewer miles between rack and delivery means lower cost per delivered gallon. (2) Demand density — terminals cluster where demand is (port, turnpike corridor, industrial base, airports), so the distributor sits inside a rare overlap of cheap supply and dense customers. In a cents-per-gallon business, that density enables route density, the single biggest controllable profit lever.
Q3A customer says "just refine us a cleaner diesel." Why is that the wrong ask, and what can a distributor actually offer instead?show answer
A distributor isn't a refiner — it can't change the molecule. What it sells is everything wrapped around the gallon: guaranteed availability, on-site and emergency delivery, tank assets, billing and tax handling, price transparency. When the fuel itself is an undifferentiated commodity, service and reliability are the only things a distributor can differentiate on.
