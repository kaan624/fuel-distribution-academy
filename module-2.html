Module 02

How a distributor operates day to day

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Walk the daily cycle: read prices → decide where to buy → price customers → dispatch → invoice.
  • Reason about OPIS rack pricing — its timings, the rack-plus formula, and why 6 PM matters.
  • Tell apart contract vs. spot on both the buying and selling sides.
  • Explain hedging at a working level, and name the three spreads that drive daily decisions.

The daily cycle

A distributor's day is a loop:

  • Read the market. NYMEX moved overnight → the spot market reacted → rack prices reset at 6 PM ET. OPIS also publishes frozen intraday snapshots through the day, so a buyer can see which rack is cheapest right now.
  • Decide where to buy. Load at the contracted terminal, or is another rack cheap enough today to justify buying there? A spread decision.
  • Price the customers. Floating accounts reprice automatically off today's rack; fixed-price accounts and live bids were locked earlier — that's where price risk lives.
  • Dispatch. Build truck routes: which deliveries, what order, which loading point, which truck. Density decided here becomes tomorrow's margin.
  • Deliver & capture data. The meter ticket — gallons, time, signature — is the atom that feeds invoicing, tax, and margin.
  • Invoice & collect. In a thin-margin business, how fast cash comes back matters as much as margin.

OPIS rack pricing, mechanically

There is no single "diesel price" at a terminal. There's a grid: ULSD from supplier A, ULSD from supplier B, dyed diesel, biodiesel blends, gasoline grades — each with its own posted number, terminal-specific and supplier-specific.

Contracts are almost never a flat price. They're written as OPIS rack ± a differential, so both sides trust an independent benchmark. A supply contract might read "OPIS Linden ULSD + 1.5¢"; the customer contract might read "OPIS + 9¢ delivered." The gap between those two formulas, minus delivery cost, is the margin.

Buy (supply contract)OPIS rack + 1.5¢
Sell (customer contract)OPIS rack + 9.0¢
Gross spread7.5¢ / gal
Delivery cost− ~4–5¢ / gal
Net margin~2.5–3.5¢ / gal

OPIS also publishes at different timings — Contract, Closing, Calendar Day. Which timing a contract references changes the number, so it's a real spec detail, never a footnote.

Contract vs. spot — on both sides

There are two separate contract-vs-spot choices, and people conflate them.

Buying (procurement)

  • Term / contract supply — committed relationship at a terminal, rack + a small differential, often with volume commitments. Predictable, secures access.
  • Spot purchase — opportunistic buying at whichever terminal is cheapest today. Flexible, captures spreads, but no guaranteed access in a tight market.

Selling

  • Fixed-price — a set price for a period. Customer loves the certainty; the distributor takes the price risk.
  • Rack-plus / floating — OPIS + differential, repriced continuously. Margin is protected because cost and price move together. Safer for the distributor.
  • Spot sale — a one-off at today's quote (most emergency and construction work is effectively this).

The tension in one line: customers want fixed; distributors want floating. A fixed-price win that isn't hedged is a bet on the market.

Spreads — what actually drives decisions

  • Location spread (NY vs NJ rack) — same product, different price at different terminals on the same day. Drives where you buy — and, later, how you bid.
  • Product spread — diesel vs. gasoline, clear vs. dyed, ULSD vs. a biodiesel blend. Drives which mix is most profitable to push.
  • Time spread — today's rack vs. the futures curve. Relevant to holding inventory or hedging.

A distributor can't move the benchmark, but it can pick the cheapest rack and the best product mix every single day. That's a data problem — the kind automation eats.

Hedging, at a working level

Hedging uses a financial instrument (usually a futures contract or swap referencing NYMEX ULSD) to offset physical price risk. Sign a fixed-price contract for 200,000 gallons; if rack prices climb, the physical deal loses money — but a futures position that gains when prices rise offsets it. Net margin is preserved either way.

Futures/swaps lock the price (no premium, but you give up the upside too). Options insure — you pay a premium for downside protection while keeping the upside. Most distributors hedging a fixed-price book use futures/swaps because they want to neutralize margin completely.

For a small-to-mid distributor, the realistic move is often to avoid the risk rather than hedge it — sell rack-plus wherever possible and only take fixed-price exposure they can cover. The sharp question is rarely "do we run a hedge book?" — it's "how much naked fixed-price exposure are we carrying right now, and do we even know?"

Basis risk (worth the term): hedges reference NYMEX, but real cost is the local rack, which doesn't move in perfect lockstep. That leftover gap is basis risk.

Key terms
OPIS timings
Contract / Closing / Calendar Day benchmarks — which one a contract cites changes the price.
Rack ± differential
The standard pricing formula: benchmark plus or minus a fixed margin.
Term supply vs. spot purchase
Committed buying relationship vs. opportunistic cheapest-rack buying.
Fixed-price vs. rack-plus
Locked price (distributor holds risk) vs. floating price (risk passed through).
Spread
A price difference — by location, product, or time — that drives buying and bidding decisions.
Hedging
Using futures/swaps (or options) to offset physical price risk on a fixed-price book.
Basis risk
The gap between the traded benchmark (NYMEX) and the price you actually pay (local rack).
Check your understanding
Q1A distributor wins a 6-month fixed-price municipal contract and does nothing else. Rack prices rise 15%. What happened, and what two tools (one commercial, one financial) could have prevented it?show answer
The rise eats straight into margin — cost climbed while the sell price was locked. Commercial fix: sell rack-plus (floating) so price tracks cost. Financial fix: hedge with a futures/swap that gains as prices rise. (Options are the pricier "insurance" variant that keeps the upside.)
Q2The NJ rack posts ULSD 2¢ cheaper than NY, but the supply contract is at the NY terminal. Name two things to weigh before switching today's load.show answer
(1) Incremental logistics cost — extra miles, time, wages, fuel: on ~8,000 gal, 2¢ ≈ $160 of saving, so does the detour beat it? (2) Access & commitments — does the distributor even have a lifting agreement and the right product at the NJ rack, and would diverting volume breach a minimum-volume commitment on the term contract?
Q3Why write contracts as "OPIS + X¢" instead of a flat price? Give the benefit to both sides.show answer
Seller: commodity risk passes through automatically — the margin (the +X¢) is preserved no matter what crude does, with no renegotiation. Buyer: trust and auditability — an independent third party sets the number, so price changes are verifiable and invoices reconcile. The neutrality of the benchmark is the load-bearing piece.
Q4A customer demands fixed pricing and refuses rack-plus; the distributor doesn't hedge. In one word, what is the distributor doing?show answer
Speculating. An unhedged fixed-price book means the distributor has taken a directional bet on diesel — it's running a commodity trading position on the side, usually without a trader's systems or discipline.
