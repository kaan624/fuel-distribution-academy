Module 03

Unit economics: where cents-per-gallon becomes profit

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Build the per-load P&L of a delivery and see where the few cents of margin actually go.
  • Separate fixed trip costs from variable per-gallon costs — and explain why that split is everything.
  • Show why a full 8,000-gallon drop can be wildly profitable while a 500-gallon top-off loses money.
  • Explain route density as the single biggest controllable profit lever.

Start from the only number that matters: margin per gallon

A fuel distributor's gross margin is measured in cents per gallon (CPG), not percent. Industry-wide, distributor profit runs roughly 3–6¢/gal — and the high end is rare. On a $3.75 gallon, a 4¢ margin is barely 1%. That single fact dictates everything that follows: you cannot earn your way out of a bad delivery on price; you earn it on volume and efficiency.

The trick: most delivery cost is fixed per trip, not per gallon

Think of every delivery as having two cost buckets:

  • Fixed per trip — driver hours, truck wear, insurance, the drive to the rack and to the site. This cost is roughly the same whether the truck drops 8,000 gallons or 500. It's the cost of showing up.
  • Variable per gallon — the fuel itself (passed through) and a little handling.

Because the trip cost is fixed, the cost per gallon delivered collapses as the drop size grows — you're spreading the same trip cost over more gallons.

FULL DROP8,000 gal · trip cost $260
Trip cost / gallon3.3¢
Gross spread earned7.5¢
Net / gallon+4.2¢ → +$336 load
SMALL TOP-OFF500 gal · trip cost $180
Trip cost / gallon36¢
Gross spread earned7.5¢
Net / gallon−28.5¢ → −$143 load

Same truck, same product, same margin formula — one load makes money, the other torches it. (Figures are illustrative, but the shape is exactly right.) This is why distributors care so much about minimum drop sizes, delivery surcharges on small loads, and full-truck routing.

Route density: the real lever

Now extend trip cost across a day. A truck and driver cost roughly the same for an 8-hour shift no matter how it's used. So profit per shift depends on how many profitable gallons that truck can deliver before the clock runs out. Two things drive that:

  • Stops close together (density) — less time driving between drops means more drops per shift.
  • Big drops per stop — more gallons per stop spreads the fixed cost.

This is why the northern-NJ geography from Module 1 is a moat: dense customers near the rack let a distributor pack a shift with large, close-together drops. The same truck that's wildly profitable on a tight urban route loses money running one small top-off 40 miles out.

Where this points the technology

Because the economics live in fixed-cost-per-trip and route density, the highest-leverage tooling is anything that (a) raises drop sizes, (b) increases stops per shift, or (c) prevents the money-losing small/emergency run before it's dispatched. Tank-level monitoring that lets you deliver a full drop just before a customer runs dry — instead of an emergency splash-and-dash — is the canonical example. We'll return to it in Modules 8 and 10.

Key terms
Cents per gallon (CPG)
The unit margin is measured in, typically 3–6¢ gross for a distributor.
Fixed trip cost
The cost of dispatching a truck (driver, drive time, wear) — roughly constant regardless of drop size.
Drop size
Gallons delivered at one stop; the bigger it is, the lower the trip cost per gallon.
Route density
How tightly stops cluster; high density = more profitable gallons per shift.
Minimum drop / small-load surcharge
Rules and fees that stop small deliveries from destroying margin.
Check your understanding
Q1Two deliveries, same 7.5¢ gross spread: one drops 8,000 gal, one drops 500 gal. Why can the small one lose money when the margin formula is identical?show answer
Because the fixed trip cost (driver, drive time, truck) is roughly the same for both, but it's spread over 16× fewer gallons on the small drop. At 500 gal the trip cost per gallon dwarfs the 7.5¢ spread, so net margin goes negative. Margin formula is identical; cost absorption is not.
Q2Why is margin measured in cents per gallon rather than percent — and what does that force the business to compete on?show answer
Because the product is a pass-through commodity: the distributor adds a small fixed markup, not a percentage of a price it controls. With ~1% effective margin, you can't win on price — you compete on volume and efficiency (route density, drop size, reliability), not on marking the gallon up.
Q3A salesperson lands a new account 35 miles from the rack that orders 600 gallons every two weeks. Why might the owner be unhappy despite "new revenue"?show answer
It's a structurally unprofitable load: small drop + long haul = very high trip cost per gallon, almost certainly below break-even at a normal spread. New revenue isn't new profit. The fix is a minimum drop size, a small-load/delivery surcharge, or routing it only when a truck is already passing nearby.
