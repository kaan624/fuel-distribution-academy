Module 04

Product & service lines

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Explain how each line — bulk delivery, mobile fleet fueling, emergency, tank sales & leasing, DEF, and aviation — actually makes money.
  • Compare the lines on margin, volume, capital intensity, and operational complexity.
  • See why the premium and recurring lines change the unit-economics math from Module 3.

A distributor isn't one business — it's a portfolio of service lines that share trucks, a back office, and a supply position but earn very differently. The trap is treating them all as "selling diesel." The skill is knowing which line earns a premium, which one just moves volume, and which one quietly locks in the others.

Bulk delivery — the volume engine

The core line: load ~8,000 gallons at the rack, deliver into a customer's on-site storage tank. This is where route density and drop size from Module 3 live or die. Margin per gallon is thin (a few cents), so bulk only works at scale and with tight routing. Think of it as the base load — high volume, low margin, the line that justifies the trucks and the supply contract.

How it makes money: volume × a small CPG spread, optimized by keeping trucks full and routes dense. A construction site taking 7,500-gallon drops twice a week is a great bulk account; a small shop taking 400 gallons once a month is a margin drain unless it's on the way to something bigger.

Mobile / on-site fleet fueling — selling the labor, not just the fuel

Instead of delivering into a tank, the truck fuels each vehicle in a customer's fleet directly — usually overnight, while trucks sit idle in the yard ("wet hosing"). The customer's drivers arrive to full tanks and never visit a retail station.

How it makes money: a premium per gallon (often several cents above bulk) plus the value of labor saved — the customer doesn't pay drivers to detour to a gas station, doesn't run its own fuel island, and gets clean per-vehicle fuel records for IFTA and cost tracking. The distributor is really selling convenience and recordkeeping, priced into the gallon. Operationally it's more complex: night routes, per-asset metering, more handling per gallon than a single bulk drop.

Emergency / mission-critical — selling certainty

Fuel delivered on demand to keep something running that cannot stop: hospital and data-center backup generators, emergency services, critical infrastructure during a storm or outage. This is the line that turns Superstorm-Sandy-type events into the most profitable week of the year.

How it makes money: a large premium for guaranteed response — the customer is paying for the promise that a truck shows up at 3 a.m. in a blizzard, not just for the gallons. From Module 3's lens this looks like a "bad" delivery (small drop, off-route, odd hours) but the premium is sized to more than cover it. The strategic point: reliability priced correctly is the highest-margin thing a distributor sells, and it's the relationship that wins the boring bulk contract later.

Tank sales & leasing — the line that locks in the others

The distributor sells or leases on-site fuel storage tanks (and the monitoring on them) to customers. On its own, modest revenue. Strategically, it's the most underrated line.

How it makes money: direct lease/sale revenue, but the real value is the switching cost it creates. A customer with the distributor's tank on their yard — often with the distributor's tank-monitor feeding the distributor's dispatch — is far stickier and naturally buys their fuel from whoever owns/services that tank. Tank leasing converts a price-shopping bulk customer into a captive recurring one. It's a moat disguised as a hardware line.

DEF — the consumable that rides along

Diesel Exhaust Fluid (urea solution) is injected into modern diesel engines' emissions systems and gets consumed at roughly 2–3% of diesel volume. Every modern diesel fleet needs it constantly.

How it makes money: a separate margin on a product the customer already needs, delivered on the same trucks to the same accounts — near-zero incremental acquisition cost. DEF is the classic attach/cross-sell: low drama, steady margin, and it deepens the account relationship. Delivered in bulk, totes, or jugs depending on fleet size.

Aviation — a different game on the same chassis

Jet fuel (Jet-A) and avgas to airports, FBOs (fixed-base operators), and aircraft operators. Higher-value product, stricter handling and quality specs, specialized equipment and certifications, and its own pricing world.

How it makes money: higher absolute margins per gallon than road diesel, but gated by certification, insurance, and quality-control requirements that make it hard to enter casually. It's a specialization, not a bolt-on — worth it where a distributor has the airport relationships and the compliance muscle.

The owner's real question for any account isn't "are they buying diesel?" — it's "how many lines are they on?" A construction account that takes bulk diesel, leases two tanks, buys DEF, and calls for emergency fills during a cold snap is worth many times a pure bulk account of the same gallons, because the premium lines carry the margin and the tank lease keeps them from leaving.

Key terms
Wet hosing
On-site mobile fueling where the distributor's truck fuels each vehicle in a fleet directly, usually overnight in the customer's yard.
Attach / cross-sell
Selling an additional product (e.g. DEF) to an existing fuel account at near-zero incremental acquisition cost.
Switching cost
The friction that keeps a customer from leaving — e.g. a leased on-site tank and monitor tied to the distributor.
FBO (fixed-base operator)
An airport-based business authorized to service aircraft, including fueling — a key aviation-fuel customer.
Mission-critical fuel
Emergency supply to operations that cannot lose power (hospitals, data centers, emergency services); priced for guaranteed response.
Check your understanding
Q1Two accounts buy the same 10,000 gallons/month of diesel. One is pure bulk; the other also leases a tank, buys DEF, and uses emergency fills. Why is the second worth far more than the gallons suggest?show answer
Because the premium and recurring lines carry the margin, not the bulk gallons. Emergency and DEF earn well above bulk CPG, and the leased tank creates a switching cost that makes the whole relationship sticky. Same gallons, completely different profit and retention profile — you're measuring the account by line count and margin mix, not volume.
Q2Why can an emergency delivery that looks terrible by Module 3's rules (tiny drop, off-route, 3 a.m.) still be the most profitable run of the month?show answer
Because the customer is paying a large premium for guaranteed response, not for the gallons. The price is sized to cover the ugly logistics and then some — they're buying certainty. It's the inverse of the small-top-off problem: a small off-route drop is a loser at bulk pricing but a winner at emergency pricing.
Q3Tank leasing is low direct revenue. Why might an owner push it hard anyway?show answer
Because it's a moat. A customer with the distributor's tank and monitor on their yard naturally buys fuel from whoever owns and services that tank, and faces friction to switch. Leasing quietly converts price-shopping bulk customers into captive recurring ones — the strategic value dwarfs the lease revenue.
