Module 05

Pricing & bid strategy

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Read OPIS / rack pricing and the NY-vs-NJ spread the way a buyer does.
  • Build a defensible quote from the ground up: cost basis + delivery cost + target margin + risk.
  • Understand how competitive bids and RFPs (transit, municipal) are won without giving away margin.

Pricing is where Modules 2 and 3 collide into a single number you hand a customer. Get it wrong high and you lose the bid; get it wrong low and you win unprofitable volume. The discipline is building the price from real components instead of guessing a markup.

Anatomy of a quote

Every defensible delivered price is built from four layers:

Cost basisrack price (OPIS + terminal) + supply differential
Taxes & feesfederal + state fuel taxes, passed through
Delivery costper-gallon trip cost — drop size & density
Target margin + riskyour CPG + a cushion for price risk

The insight from Module 3 reappears here: the same gallon costs a different amount to deliver to different customers. A dense, full-truck account can be quoted tighter and still be profitable; a small, far account must be quoted higher or declined. Pricing is account-specific, not a single list price.

OPIS / rack mechanics, applied

Recall from Module 2: contracts are written as OPIS rack ± differential, not flat. When you quote a recurring customer, you're usually quoting your differential — the cents above the benchmark — because the benchmark itself floats. This is why an experienced buyer negotiates the differential and the OPIS timing reference, not "the price of diesel," which neither party controls.

Which OPIS timing the contract cites (Contract / Closing / Calendar Day) and which terminal's rack it references are real levers. A contract pinned to a favorable terminal benchmark can be worth more than a slightly better differential.

The NY vs NJ spread as a weapon

The location spread from Module 2 isn't just a sourcing decision — it's a bidding edge. If the NJ rack consistently prices below the NY rack for the product a customer needs, a NJ-based distributor lifting at NJ terminals has a structurally lower cost basis than a competitor pricing off NY. That lets it either win on price or pocket wider margin at the same price. Knowing the spread, and which terminal each competitor lifts from, is genuine competitive intelligence.

New Jersey taxes, concretely

NJ fuel taxes are among the highest in the country and they move every year, so they're a live part of every quote. As of January 2026:

PPGRT on diesel42.6¢ / gal
Motor Fuels Tax on diesel (fixed)13.5¢ / gal
Combined NJ state tax on diesel56.1¢ / gal
(Gasoline combined, for reference)49.1¢ / gal

Two practical consequences. First, dyed (off-road) diesel is exempt — so correctly classifying a construction or generator account as off-road isn't a technicality, it's a large price difference that can win or lose a bid. Second, because the PPGRT resets annually by formula, any multi-year fixed quote that doesn't account for tax changes is taking on hidden risk. Build the tax as an explicit pass-through line, not a baked-in number.

Winning competitive bids & RFPs

Public and large private contracts (transit agencies, municipalities, large fleets) are won through formal bids. The keys:

  • Bid the differential, index the base. Almost all serious fuel RFPs are structured as OPIS + a bid differential, precisely so the agency isn't betting on the market and can audit every invoice. You're competing on your differential and your service terms, not a flat price.
  • Price to the route, not the logo. A municipal contract that clusters with your existing accounts can be bid aggressively because the marginal delivery cost is low. The same contract isolated 40 miles out cannot. Density wins bids profitably.
  • Win the premium lines around the commodity. The bulk gallons may be near break-even at competitive-bid differentials; the margin comes from the emergency, DEF, and tank-lease attachments the relationship opens (Module 4).
  • Compliance and reliability are scored. Public bids weight on-time performance, insurance, environmental record, and references — not just price. A distributor that can document reliability can win without being the cheapest.

The classic mistake: winning a big municipal bid on a thin differential, then discovering the deliveries are small, scattered, and off-route — turning a marquee logo into a money-loser. Always model the actual delivery pattern before bidding the differential.

Key terms
Differential
The cents above (or below) the OPIS benchmark that a distributor charges or pays — the real number negotiated in an indexed contract.
Indexed (OPIS-plus) bid
An RFP structured as OPIS benchmark + a bid differential, so the buyer carries no market risk and can audit invoices.
Pass-through
A cost (e.g. fuel tax) charged to the customer at cost as an explicit line, not absorbed into margin.
Cost basis
The all-in cost of the fuel you'll deliver: rack price + supply differential + taxes, before delivery cost and margin.
Dyed-fuel exemption
Off-road (red-dyed) diesel is exempt from highway fuel taxes — correctly classifying an account materially changes its price.
Check your understanding
Q1A competitor based near the NY harbor and you, based in NJ, bid the same municipal diesel contract. The NJ rack runs 2-3¢ under NY. Where does that show up, and how do you use it?show answer
It shows up in your cost basis — you lift cheaper, so at the same delivered price you earn a wider margin, or you can bid a tighter differential and still profit. You use it by either undercutting on the differential to win, or matching their price and banking the spread. Knowing which terminal a competitor lifts from is real competitive intelligence.
Q2Why do serious fuel RFPs ask for a differential over OPIS instead of a fixed price per gallon — and what does that mean you're actually competing on?show answer
So the buyer carries no commodity risk and can audit every invoice against an independent benchmark (the Module 2 point). It means you're competing on your differential and service terms — reliability, response, references — not on a flat price neither side controls. The benchmark floats; your differential and service are the contest.
Q3A salesperson is thrilled to win a city contract on a razor-thin differential. As the owner, what do you check before celebrating?show answer
The actual delivery pattern: drop sizes, locations, frequency, and whether it clusters with existing routes. A thin differential is fine on dense, full-truck drops and a disaster on small, scattered, off-route ones (Module 3). Also check what premium attachments (emergency, DEF, tank lease) the relationship opens, since that's where the real margin will come from.
