Module 06

Customers & segments

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Profile each segment — construction, logistics, rail, municipal, marine, aviation — by buying behavior and contract type.
  • Map account lifecycles, especially the construction ramp-and-taper.
  • Connect segment mix to the volume, density, and margin that drive profit.

Every segment buys fuel, but they buy it for different reasons, on different terms, with different lifecycles and different sensitivities. Reading the segment tells you how to price it, how stable it is, and how to keep it.

Construction — high volume, finite life

Job sites running heavy equipment and generators, often on dyed off-road diesel. Volume can be large and deliveries dense while a project is active.

  • Buying behavior: driven by the project schedule, not the calendar. Price-sensitive but reliability-sensitive too — equipment downtime is expensive.
  • Contract type: often spot or short-term; tied to the life of the job.
  • Lifecycle — the ramp-and-taper: volume climbs as the site mobilizes, peaks mid-project, then tapers to zero when the job ends. This is the defining construction dynamic: today's biggest account can be gone in six months. A distributor over-indexed on construction has volume that looks great until several projects wrap at once.

The strategic takeaway: construction volume is real but non-recurring. It must be continuously replaced with new projects, and it shouldn't be mistaken for a stable base. Track it as a pipeline, not an annuity.

Logistics & trucking fleets — the recurring base

Trucking companies, distribution centers, and delivery fleets that consume diesel continuously and predictably. The prime customer for mobile/on-site fueling (Module 4).

  • Buying behavior: steady, volume-driven, highly price-aware on the commodity but very willing to pay for convenience and clean fuel records (IFTA reporting).
  • Contract type: recurring contracts, often indexed (OPIS-plus), frequently bundled with on-site fueling and DEF.
  • Lifecycle: stable and renewable — this is the recurring base that offsets construction's volatility.

Rail — large, infrastructural, sticky

Railroads and rail-yard operations consuming large diesel volumes at fixed locations.

  • Buying behavior: volume-dominated, relationship- and reliability-driven, slow to switch.
  • Contract type: larger, longer contracts; significant logistics commitment.
  • Lifecycle: very sticky once won — high switching cost, infrastructural.

Municipal & government — bid-driven, stable, slow-paying

Cities, counties, transit agencies, school districts, public fleets. Often the source of the marquee contracts (NJ Transit-type accounts).

  • Buying behavior: procurement-driven and price-disciplined, but stable and creditworthy. Insists on auditable OPIS-indexed pricing.
  • Contract type: formal RFP / competitive bid, multi-year, indexed differentials.
  • Lifecycle: stable across the contract term, then re-bid — you can lose it at renewal on price even after years of good service.
  • Watch the A/R: governments pay reliably but slowly — long payment terms tie up cash (Module 9). Stable revenue, demanding working capital.

Marine / port — specialized, premium

Vessels, port operations, and marine equipment, especially relevant in the NY/NJ port complex. Specialized delivery (barge or truck-to-vessel), often premium.

  • Buying behavior: reliability- and logistics-driven; specialized handling.
  • Contract type: mix of contract and spot; premium for specialized delivery.
  • Lifecycle: relationship-driven, sticky where the logistics are hard to replicate.

Aviation — gated and high-value

Airports, FBOs, and aircraft operators (Module 4). High value per gallon, high compliance bar.

  • Buying behavior: quality- and certification-driven; less price-elastic.
  • Contract type: specialized supply agreements.
  • Lifecycle: sticky where certification and relationships are established.

Reading the portfolio

The healthiest book balances recurring base (logistics, rail, municipal) against high-volume but finite (construction) and premium specialized (marine, aviation, emergency). The danger signs an owner watches: too much concentration in construction (volume cliff risk), too much municipal without watching A/R (cash-flow strain), or a base that's all thin-margin bulk with no premium attachments.

Key terms
Ramp-and-taper
The construction-account lifecycle: volume rises as a site mobilizes, peaks, then falls to zero as the project ends. Non-recurring.
Recurring base
Stable, renewable accounts (logistics, rail, municipal) that provide predictable volume and offset construction volatility.
Account concentration
Over-reliance on one segment or customer — e.g. too much construction creates volume-cliff risk.
Re-bid risk
The chance of losing a municipal/government contract at renewal on price, regardless of service quality.
Creditworthy but slow
Customers (especially government) who pay reliably but on long terms, tying up working capital.
Check your understanding
Q1A distributor's three largest accounts are all construction sites, and revenue looks fantastic. What's the risk an owner sees that the salesperson might not?show answer
The volume cliff. Construction is ramp-and-taper and non-recurring — when those projects wrap (possibly close together), the biggest accounts go to zero with little warning. Great-looking revenue built on construction is a pipeline that must be constantly refilled, not a stable base. The fix is balancing it with recurring logistics/rail/municipal volume.
Q2Municipal contracts are stable and creditworthy. Name two ways they can still strain the business.show answer
(1) Slow payment — governments pay reliably but on long terms, tying up working capital and stretching DSO (Module 9). (2) Re-bid risk — the contract is competitively re-bid at renewal, so years of good service can be lost on price. Stable revenue, but demanding on cash and never permanently "won."
Q3Why are logistics/trucking fleets often the most valuable segment to build around, even though they're price-aware on the commodity?show answer
Because they're a recurring, renewable base (offsetting construction's volatility) and they're the prime customer for the premium attachments — on-site mobile fueling and DEF — where the real margin lives. They'll pay for convenience and clean IFTA records even while pushing on the commodity price, so the relationship carries margin beyond the thin bulk spread.
