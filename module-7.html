Module 07

Regulation & compliance, practically

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Navigate the EPA / environmental rules that touch a distributor day to day.
  • Understand Hazmat transport requirements for moving fuel by truck.
  • Handle fuel/excise taxes and IFTA, plus tank and SPCC obligations — and see where each creates operational work that software can absorb.

Compliance in this business isn't abstract paperwork — it's woven into every gallon, every truck, and every tank. The practical lens: each rule creates recurring operational work (tracking, reporting, documentation), and that work is exactly where errors get expensive and where automation pays off.

Fuel & excise taxes — the everyday compliance load

Fuel is one of the most heavily taxed commodities, and the distributor sits at the collection point. Practically:

  • Federal + state taxes apply per gallon and must be correctly applied, collected, and remitted. In NJ the combined state tax on diesel is 56.1¢/gal (2026), and the PPGRT portion resets annually by formula — so rate tables must be kept current.
  • Dyed vs. clear classification is a tax-critical distinction: dyed (off-road) diesel is tax-exempt, clear (highway) is taxed. Misclassifying a delivery is a tax error, not a cosmetic one.
  • Exemptions and certificates — some customers (certain government, agricultural, off-road uses) are exempt and must have valid exemption documentation on file. Tracking certificate validity is ongoing work.

IFTA — the interstate trucking wrinkle

The International Fuel Tax Agreement governs how interstate truckers pay fuel taxes apportioned by miles driven in each state. A distributor intersects IFTA in two ways:

  • Its own fleet — if the distributor's trucks cross state lines (e.g. NJ/NY/PA), the company files IFTA reports apportioning fuel tax by jurisdiction, based on mileage and fuel records per truck.
  • Its customers' need for data — fleet-fueling customers rely on clean per-vehicle fuel records to do their IFTA reporting. This is part of why on-site fueling's recordkeeping (Module 4) is worth a premium: the distributor is producing IFTA-grade data as a byproduct.

IFTA is fundamentally a data-reconciliation problem: miles per state × fuel per state, per truck, per quarter. It's tedious and error-prone by hand — a clean example of compliance work that's really a data pipeline.

Hazmat transport — moving the product legally

Diesel and especially gasoline are hazardous materials in transport, regulated federally (DOT/PHMSA). Practically this means:

  • Driver qualifications — CDL with hazmat and tanker endorsements, and ongoing training/certification.
  • Vehicle & equipment standards — compliant tankers, placarding, inspection regimes.
  • Documentation — shipping papers, manifests, and records that must travel with the load and be retained.
  • Safety & incident protocols — spill response, reporting obligations.

The recurring work here is credential and inspection tracking: which drivers are certified through when, which trucks are inspected through when. Letting one lapse can ground a truck or trigger a violation.

Tanks & SPCC — storing the product legally

Because the distributor sells and leases on-site tanks and stores fuel itself, it lives in the world of storage-tank regulation. The key federal rule is SPCC — Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure (40 CFR 112):

SPCC applies if aboveground storage exceeds1,320 gallons
...or completely buried storage exceeds42,000 gallons
(counting only containers ≥ 55 gal)each tank's capacity counts
PE-certified plan required above10,000 gallons total
Plan review/update cycleevery 5 years
  • A written SPCC plan is required for facilities over threshold with a reasonable chance a spill could reach navigable waters — interpreted broadly. It must be kept on site and, above 10,000 gallons, certified by a professional engineer.
  • Secondary containment (e.g. berms, double-walled tanks) is the core requirement — a way to catch a spill before it escapes.
  • Underground storage tanks (USTs) carry their own federal and state requirements (registration, leak detection, financial responsibility).
  • This matters for customers too: a customer leasing a tank may itself cross SPCC thresholds — the distributor that understands this can advise (and sell containment/monitoring), turning compliance into a service.

The throughline: compliance is recurring data work

Step back and every item above is a tracking-and-reporting loop: tax tables and remittances, exemption certificates, IFTA reconciliations, driver/truck credentials and inspections, tank inspections and plan-renewal dates. None of it is conceptually hard; all of it is error-prone, deadline-driven, and expensive to get wrong. That makes compliance one of the clearest places where good systems and automation create real, defensible value — the theme Module 8 picks up.

Key terms
PPGRT
NJ Petroleum Products Gross Receipts Tax — the diesel portion is 42.6¢/gal (2026) and resets annually by formula.
Dyed vs. clear diesel
Dyed = off-road, tax-exempt; clear = highway, taxed. Misclassification is a tax error.
Exemption certificate
Documentation proving a customer's tax-exempt status; validity must be tracked.
IFTA
International Fuel Tax Agreement — interstate fuel tax apportioned by miles per state, reported quarterly per truck.
Hazmat endorsements
CDL credentials (hazmat + tanker) required to legally transport fuel; must be kept current.
SPCC
EPA Spill Prevention, Control & Countermeasure rule; triggers at 1,320 gal aboveground / 42,000 buried; requires a spill plan and secondary containment.
Secondary containment
A barrier (berm, double wall) that catches a spill before it can reach waterways — core SPCC requirement.
UST
Underground storage tank — separately regulated (registration, leak detection, financial responsibility).

Q1Why is the dyed-vs-clear diesel distinction a compliance issue and not just a product detail?show answer
Because dyed (off-road) diesel is tax-exempt and clear (highway) diesel is taxed (56.1¢/gal in NJ). Delivering one and billing/classifying it as the other is a tax error with real liability — under-collecting taxed fuel, or improperly using exempt fuel on-road. Correct classification per delivery is a compliance obligation, not bookkeeping.
Q2A customer wants to lease a 2,000-gallon aboveground diesel tank for their yard near a creek. What compliance flag should the distributor raise — and how is that also a sales opportunity?show answer
2,000 gallons aboveground is over the SPCC 1,320-gallon threshold, and proximity to a waterway makes a discharge plausible — so the customer likely needs an SPCC plan and secondary containment. That's a compliance flag, but also a chance to sell containment, monitoring, and advisory service: the distributor turns a regulatory burden into a value-added offering and deepens the account.
Q3In what sense is IFTA 'really a data pipeline,' and why does that connect to the premium on on-site fueling?show answer
IFTA is miles-per-state × fuel-per-state, per truck, per quarter — a tedious, error-prone reconciliation, not a hard concept. On-site fleet fueling produces clean per-vehicle fuel records as a byproduct, which is exactly the IFTA-grade data the customer needs. So the distributor can charge a premium partly because it's solving the customer's data/compliance problem, not just delivering gallons.
