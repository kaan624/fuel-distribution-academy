Module 09

Key metrics & KPIs an owner watches

By the end of this module you'll be able to
  • Define the core KPIs: gallons, margin per gallon, on-time %, account volume trends, A/R and DSO.
  • Connect each metric back to the four levers and the unit economics.
  • Read a distributor's dashboard the way an owner does — what's healthy, what's an early warning.

Everything in the prior modules rolls up into a handful of numbers an owner watches. The skill isn't memorizing metrics — it's knowing which lever each one reflects and which ones are leading (warn early) versus lagging (confirm what already happened).

Volume metrics — gallons

  • Total gallons delivered (by period, segment, product) — the top-line activity measure. But raw gallons can mislead: gallons from a construction project that's about to taper aren't the same as gallons from a recurring fleet (Module 6).
  • Gallons by segment & by account — reveals concentration risk (too much construction) and account-level trends.

Profitability metrics — margin per gallon

  • Margin per gallon (CPG) — the single most important profitability number (Module 3). Watched in aggregate and by account/segment, because a high-volume account at near-zero CPG can be worth less than a smaller premium one.
  • Margin mix — how much profit comes from premium lines (emergency, DEF, mobile, tank lease) vs. thin bulk (Module 4). Healthy books earn disproportionately from the premium attachments.
  • Gross profit dollars — CPG × gallons; the number that actually pays the bills. The discipline is holding both up at once: chasing gallons at collapsing CPG, or CPG at collapsing volume, both fail.

Operational metrics — efficiency & reliability

  • On-time delivery % — the core reliability KPI. Reliability is what the premium lines are sold on (Module 4); a slipping on-time rate erodes the thing customers pay extra for and shows up later as churn.
  • Route density / drops per truck-day / average drop size — direct readouts of the Module 3 profit engine. Falling drop size or density means margin is leaking even if gallons hold.
  • Emergency vs. planned delivery ratio — too many emergency runs signals broken predictive routing (Module 8) — reactive, expensive, and a sign data isn't flowing.

Account-health metrics — trends & retention

  • Account volume trends — rising/flat/declining per account. A quietly declining anchor account is an early churn warning; a construction account's taper should be anticipated, not discovered.
  • Account concentration — share of revenue/gallons in the top handful of accounts or one segment. High concentration = fragility (Module 6).
  • Line count per account — how many service lines each account is on (Module 4); a leading indicator of stickiness and margin.

Cash metrics — the fourth lever

In a thin-margin business, cash timing can matter as much as margin. This is where the fourth controllable lever — how fast you collect — is measured.

A/R (accounts receivable)cash owed but not yet collected
DSO (days sales outstanding)avg. days to get paid
Aging buckets0-30 / 31-60 / 61-90 / 90+ days
The squeezeyou pay the rack now, customers pay you later
  • DSO — average days to collect. Rising DSO ties up working capital and can choke a profitable-on-paper business. Municipal accounts (Module 6) reliably push DSO up.
  • A/R aging — how much is overdue and how badly. The 90+ bucket is where bad debt risk lives.
  • The structural squeeze: a distributor often pays for fuel at the rack within days but collects from customers in weeks — so growth itself consumes cash. Watching DSO and aging isn't optional bookkeeping; it's survival.

Reading the dashboard like an owner

The owner's mental scan: Are gross profit dollars holding (CPG × gallons)? Is margin mix tilting to premium or eroding to bulk? Is on-time % steady? Are any anchor accounts quietly declining or over-concentrated? Is DSO creeping up? The leading indicators — drop size, on-time %, account trends, emergency ratio, DSO — warn before the lagging financials confirm a problem. A distributor that only watches gallons and revenue is driving by looking in the mirror.

Tie-back: nearly every KPI here maps to a lever or a unit-economics fact from earlier modules. Margin/gal and drop size = Module 3. Margin mix and line count = Module 4. Concentration and re-bid = Module 6. Emergency ratio and integration = Module 8. DSO = the cash lever from Module 1. The dashboard is just the whole course expressed as numbers.

Key terms
Margin per gallon (CPG)
Gross profit divided by gallons; the central profitability metric, watched in aggregate and per account.
Margin mix
The share of profit from premium lines vs. thin bulk; healthy books lean on premium attachments.
Gross profit dollars
CPG × gallons — the absolute profit that pays the bills; must be held up on both factors at once.
On-time delivery %
Core reliability KPI; erosion undermines the premium customers pay for and predicts churn.
Account concentration
Share of revenue in the top accounts or one segment; high concentration means fragility.
A/R (accounts receivable)
Money owed by customers but not yet collected.
DSO (days sales outstanding)
Average days to collect payment; rising DSO ties up working capital.
A/R aging
Breakdown of receivables by how overdue they are (0-30, 31-60, 61-90, 90+); the 90+ bucket signals bad-debt risk.
Leading vs. lagging indicator
Leading metrics (drop size, on-time %, DSO) warn early; lagging metrics (revenue, net profit) confirm after the fact.
Check your understanding
Q1Two accounts each deliver 50,000 gallons this quarter. Account A is flat bulk at 3¢ CPG; Account B is 4¢ bulk plus DEF, two leased tanks, and three emergency fills. Why does an owner value B far above A despite equal gallons?show answer
Because gross profit dollars and margin mix — not gallons — are what matter. B earns more per bulk gallon, adds high-margin premium lines (DEF, emergency), and the leased tanks create switching cost. A is thin, undifferentiated, and easy to lose on price. Equal gallons, very different profit and retention. Watching gallons alone would rate them the same — a classic mistake.
Q2A distributor is profitable on paper but constantly short on cash. Which metrics explain it, and what's the structural cause?show answer
DSO and A/R aging. The structural cause is the timing squeeze: the distributor pays the rack within days but collects from customers in weeks (worse with municipal accounts), so working capital is tied up and growth consumes cash. Rising DSO and a fattening 90+ aging bucket reveal it. Profit on the P&L doesn't equal cash in the bank.
Q3Why does an owner watch leading indicators like drop size, on-time %, and emergency-run ratio rather than just revenue and net profit?show answer
Because revenue and net profit are lagging — they confirm a problem after it's happened. Falling drop size means margin is already leaking (Module 3); a slipping on-time % predicts churn before it shows in revenue; a rising emergency ratio signals broken predictive routing (Module 8). Leading indicators let the owner act before the financials catch up — otherwise you're driving by the mirror.
